## üé¨ Core Production Requirements

### Pacing & Storytelling
- **Slow story progress** - Allow the narrative to breathe and develop naturally
- **Extended shots** - Don't rush; let moments linger to build atmosphere
- **High shot count per scene** - Multiple angles to capture complete emotional arcs
- **Scene consistency** - Maintain continuity in environment, lighting, and character state
- **Character consistency** - Track wounds, dirt, exhaustion, clothing damage across timeline

### Emotional Depth
- **Provide enough time to showcase character emotions**
- Focus on subtle expressions and body language
- Show internal struggle through facial reactions
- Capture the weight of isolation and decision-making
- Allow silent moments for reflection

## üéØ Key Themes & Story Elements

### Primary Emotions & States
| Theme | Description | Visual Approach |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Loneliness** | Isolation from civilization, no human contact | Wide shots emphasizing emptiness, character dwarfed by environment |
| **Survival Instinct** | Fight to stay alive, resourcefulness | Close-ups on hands working, problem-solving sequences |
| **Desperation** | Running out of resources, hope fading | Shallow focus on exhausted face, shaky hands, labored breathing |
| **Determination** | Refusing to give up | Strong eye contact with camera, clenched fists, forward movement |
| **Hope** | Finding food, water, shelter | Soft lighting, relief in expressions, slower breathing |
| **Fear** | Predators, weather, injury | Dutch angles, shadows, rapid heartbeat visualization |

## üì∑ Camera Work & Shot Selection

### Shot Variety Requirements
**Use the FULL range from extreme close-ups to extreme wide shots**

#### Extreme Close-Ups (ECU)
- **Eyes** showing fear, determination, tears, or relief
- **Hands** working on fire, handling tools, treating wounds
- **Water** dripping from leaves, being cupped in hands
- **Food** being eaten hungrily, examined for safety
- **Insects** crawling, details of nature
- **Textures** bark, mud, sweat, blood

#### Close-Ups (CU)
- Facial expressions during emotional moments
- Reaction to discoveries (food, water, danger)
- Physical exhaustion and pain
- Moments of realization or decision

#### Medium Shots (MS)
- Character performing tasks (making fire, building shelter)
- Interaction with animals
- Eating, resting, working
- Body language showing mood and energy level

#### Wide Shots (WS)
- Character in environment showing scale
- Establishing scenes
- Journey and movement through terrain
- Shelter and campsite overviews

#### Extreme Wide Shots (EWS)
- **Emphasizing loneliness** - tiny figure in vast landscape
- Environmental grandeur (mountains, oceans, forests)
- Scale of challenge ahead
- Beauty and danger of nature
- Weather systems approaching

### Specialized Angles for Survival Stories

#### High Angle Shots
- Character looking vulnerable, overwhelmed
- Showing exhaustion or defeat
- POV from tree or cliff looking down

#### Low Angle Shots
- Character showing strength and determination
- Climbing sequences
- Facing challenges with resolve
- Animal POV looking at character

#### Bird's Eye View
- Navigation and geography
- Character's path through wilderness
- Camp layout and surroundings
- Tracking movement over time

#### Dutch/Canted Angles
- Disorientation, fever, dehydration
- Danger approaching
- Unstable emotional state
- Hallucinations

#### POV Shots
- Character's perspective while exploring
- Looking at hands while performing tasks
- View from inside shelter or cave
- Approaching water source or finding food

## üé≠ Scene Construction Guidelines

### Scene Length & Pacing
- **Don't rush scenes** - Allow 20-40% longer than typical content
- **Extend establishing shots** - Let audience absorb the environment
- **Hold on reactions** - Give emotional moments space to land
- **Use silence effectively** - Natural sounds, breathing, environment

### Shots Per Scene Recommendations

| Scene Type | Minimum Shots | Recommended | Purpose |
|------------|--------------|-------------|---------|
| Discovery (food/water) | 5-8 | 10-15 | Show searching, finding, reaction, examination, consumption |
| Building/Creating | 6-10 | 12-20 | Process steps, problem-solving, completion, satisfaction |
| Emotional Moment | 4-6 | 8-12 | Wide context, approach close-up, hold on emotion, aftermath |
| Action/Danger | 8-12 | 15-25 | Threat, reaction, action, resolution, recovery |
| Rest/Contemplation | 3-5 | 6-10 | Environment, character settling, thoughts, acceptance |
| Nature Experience | 5-8 | 10-15 | Approach, interaction, wonder, appreciation |

### Shot Duration
- **Establishing shots**: 3-6 seconds
- **Action shots**: 2-4 seconds
- **Emotional close-ups**: 3-8 seconds (can extend to 12s for key moments)
- **Contemplative wide shots**: 4-10 seconds
- **Nature beauty shots**: 5-12 seconds

## üé® Visual Style & Atmosphere

### Lighting Approach
- **Golden hour** for hope and beauty
- **Harsh midday** for struggle and difficulty
- **Overcast** for bleakness and challenge
- **Firelight** for warmth and safety
- **Moonlight** for danger and mystery
- **Dawn/Dusk** for transitions and change

### Color Palette
- **Desaturated** during hardship
- **Vibrant** during moments of joy or discovery
- **Cool tones** for water, night, danger
- **Warm tones** for fire, hope, safety

### Weather as Character
- Rain can be relief (water) or obstacle (shelter)
- Wind creates constant challenge
- Fog adds mystery and disorientation
- Sun provides warmth but risks dehydration

## üìù Story Arc Framework

### Act 1: The Situation (10-15%)
- How character ended up here
- Initial shock and disbelief
- Taking stock of resources
- First night survival

### Act 2: Adaptation (60-70%)
- Learning to survive
- Small victories and defeats
- Bonding with environment
- Physical and mental challenges
- Developing routines
- Creative problem-solving

### Act 3: Transformation (15-20%)
- Mastery of environment
- Peace with situation or rescue attempt
- Final challenge
- Resolution (rescue, escape, or acceptance)

## üéØ Production Checklist

### Pre-Production
- [ ] Scout locations for variety (forest, water, caves, open areas)
- [ ] Plan weather conditions for different emotional beats
- [ ] Design character arc and emotional progression
- [ ] Create detailed shot list with camera angles
- [ ] Plan practical effects (fire, water, weather)
- [ ] Prepare props (tools, natural elements, survival gear)

### During Production
- [ ] Capture extensive B-roll of environment
- [ ] Get multiple takes of key emotional moments
- [ ] Maintain continuity photos for character state
- [ ] Record wild sound (nature, ambience)
- [ ] Get establishing shots at different times of day
- [ ] Capture insert shots of details

### Post-Production
- [ ] Edit for pacing - slower than typical content
- [ ] Color grade to match emotional tone
- [ ] Sound design with layered nature sounds
- [ ] Add breathing, effort sounds for realism
- [ ] Music sparingly - let silence speak
- [ ] Extended sequences for key moments

---

## üí° Pro Tips

1. **Character Development Through Actions** - Show don't tell their growth
2. **Nature Sounds Matter** - Wind, water, birds create immersion
3. **Dirt & Damage Continuity** - Track character's physical state meticulously
4. **Micro-Expressions** - Capture subtle emotion changes in close-ups
5. **Environmental Storytelling** - Use background elements to show time passage
6. **Practical Effects** - Real fire, water, weather when possible
7. **Reference Real Survival** - Study actual survival situations for authenticity
8. **Give Space for Thought** - Silent moments let audience connect emotionally
